# Hacking: The Open Source Chapter

### Hacking and Open Source
From the creation of the computer in the early 19th century, to our modern day, the
definition of the term 'hacker' has shifted from a term of praise to one of malicious
intent. In the early days, a hacker was typically understood as an individual who was
knowledgeable about technology and enjoyed using their knowledge to improve the
technology. Nowadays when someone hears the word hacker, they tend to think of a person
in a black hoodie sitting behind a computer and using malicious techniques to access,
steal, or modify unauthorized data. While it is more than likely that the modern 
definition of hacker would apply to at least <em>someone</em> throughout its evolution,
the majority of hackers fall into the early days definition. In fact, the early days
definition is one of the main drivers of Open Source Software, which is any software
that has made its source code freely available to anyone and may be viewed, modified,
or redistributed. Hackers tend to have a highly favorable view of Open Source Software
because it allows for endless customization, where as closed source software denies the
user the freedom to modify anything that the developer does not want modified. This
lack of freedom in closed source software creates a sort of mysterious wall that may
not be passed, which is extremely attractive to hackers who may then find themselves
recreating the software in their own unique way as an Open Source project.

### The Growing Norm of Closed Source Through Stallman's View
Because of the expensive nature of developing software in the early days of computing,
"companies would charge for software licenses, marketing software as 'program products'
and imposing legal restrictions on new software developments." (wikipedia) This method
of restriction proved difficult to enforce, so companies began to distribute their 
software in the form of executables, or pre-compiled source code. This would require
anyone wanting to modify the source code to reverse engineer the executable, which
proves extremely difficult because crucial components of the source code are not
recoverable post-compilation. This move by software companies was disliked by the vast
majority of hobbyists and hackers, especially Richard Stallman. While many chose to
accept the reality of what software had come to be, Stallman, a lab staff system hacker
at the MIT Artificial Intelligence Lab, chose to take the road less traveled. Throughout
the 1970's and 80's, Stallman watched non-free proprietary software become the norm as
companies hired away his coworkers, and even managed to infiltrate the AI Lab, switching
the open source operating system to a non-free version. Stallman had seen enough to 
confidently decide that he didn't want to live in a closed source world and began to
contemplate what he could do. The answer turned out to be simple: GNU, an open source
operating system, of which Stallman was very familiar with. Stallman needed a concrete
way to keep his operating system in the hands of the people and away from the grasp of
corporate greed, so he founded the Free Software Foundation and later wrote the GNU 
General Public License which is copy left license for software, provided for
free, and allows anyone to do anything they'd like to the software but with one 
restriction: they themselves may not place any restrictions on the software.

### Linux
Richard Stallman set the path for open source software, and perhaps the greatest proof
lies in another open source project: the Linux kernel. Linus Torvalds released
the Linux kernel in 1991 as a solution for GNU's lack thereof, perfectly representing
Stallman's vision for the world of open source.
